# PM Philosophy

## How I Think About Product Work

I don't separate strategy from execution. The interesting PM work is in the details — how the state machine works, how the data flows, what the edge case actually is in production. PMs who only operate at the strategy layer end up shipping things that don't work the way they imagined.

## On Roadmaps

A roadmap is a hypothesis about what matters, not a commitment. The most useful roadmaps I've worked with are short, opinionated, and updated often. The least useful are long, comprehensive, and used to manage stakeholders rather than guide work.

I care more about having a clear point of view on the next three things than a detailed plan for the next three quarters.

## On Prioritization

First principles thinking matters most for novel or 0-to-1 problems. For incremental improvements on established products, pattern matching is usually faster and good enough. Knowing which situation you're in is half the job.

I use impact versus effort as a starting point, not a final answer. The missing variable is usually reversibility — how bad is it if we're wrong, and how quickly can we find out?

## On Working with Engineering

The best product-engineering relationships I've had are ones where the engineers feel ownership over the problem, not just the implementation. That means involving them earlier, being honest about uncertainty, and not pretending the spec is complete when it isn't.

I can review code, understand architecture trade-offs, and have informed opinions about technical decisions. That makes the relationship easier but it also means I need to be disciplined about when to push and when to defer.

## On Metrics

Metrics are useful for learning, not just tracking. The most important question about any metric isn't whether it went up or down — it's whether it's measuring what you actually care about. A lot of product teams optimize metrics that are easy to move rather than ones that reflect real value.

## On 0-to-1 Work

Early-stage product work requires a different operating mode than growth-stage product work. The goal is to find out you're wrong as fast as possible, not to ship something polished. I've done enough 0-to-1 work to know that the biggest risk is usually building too much before you've validated the core assumption.
